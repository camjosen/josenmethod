## Pedagogy

- Reading is a complex skill with many subskills. It’s helpful to teach the most foundational skill first, and build from there.
- Clear, unambiguous communication helps kids learn.
- English has 26 letters and 44 sounds. Letters can represent multiple sounds, making English a difficult language to read. We can use a special font that removes the ambiguity of English letters, making reading easier. This font can be “phased out” in stages until only normal letters remain. Before this phase out, letters can be referred to as sounds.
- We prefer lowercase letters because those are most commonly used in the words we see everyday. But the learner may also choose to use uppercase letters.
- It can be useful to teach patterns. For example, you might teach “smile” followed by “smiled” the look at other words where you can add a “d”.
- Some words are just plain funny. You can’t logic your way to sounding them out. That’s okay. These can be memorized.
- Sounding out words is a key skill. Guessing is an anti-skill.
- Teachers need to practice core skills so they can model them. Pronunciation of sounds and blending of sounds are critical. Inconsistency from the teacher yields confusion in the student.
- Many sounds are continuous — m, s, r, th, n f, l, sh, v, ar ,ing, er, oo, wh, qu, x, w, z, and all of the vowels and vowel combinations. These sounds tend to be easier to learn than “stop” sounds like t, c, d, g, h, k, p, ch, b, and j. Try saying “mad” slowly and notice how you cannot hold the “d” sound.
- Another dimension is “voiced vs. whispered”. The former you can feel in your vocal cords. The latter is “just breath”.
- There are regional differences in pronouncing sounds.
- When decoding words, perfection isn’t necessary. You just need to get close enough. For example, you might know how “th” sounds in “this” and “that”. When you get to words like “math” and “thing”, you might get confused for a second, but the difference in sound is small enough that it should “just click” without needing a tedious lesson on the subject. Something similar happens with “oo” in “moon” vs. “book”. Yet another interesting example is “qu”, which could be taught as sounding like “kw” but can just be “koo”, which is easier and is “good enough” for the purpose of decoding words.
- Blending is important. Avoid gaps between letters when sounding out words.
- The teacher has a script to follow for each activity.
- It can be useful to compare words that rhyme to gain familiarity with sound patterns. It’s also useful to compare words that have the same beginnings but different ends.
- Stop sounds are interesting, especially when they come at the beginning of words. The key is to teach blending that sound with the sound that comes immediately after it. Avoid saying the stop sound aloud on its own. Don’t say, “c aaannn.” Say, “caaannn”.
- When words have silent letters, those letters can receive a visual treatment to show that they should not be spoken. One option is to make them small. Another option is to grey them out.
- Unlike a video game where you “just play”, learning can be hard work, and it often makes sense to help students mentally prepare for that work. Instructions for active listening, for example, can be helpful.
- There’s an art to giving feedback. Try to keep it concrete and specific so that the child can connect their decisions to the feedback. Generic praise like “good work” is often too difficult to connect to choices made by the student.
- Correct mistakes immediately.

# Dependencies that affect lesson design

- Don't use things that haven't been introduced. This includes:
  - words with new sounds
  - words with silent sounds
  - capital letters
  - multi-syllable words
  - funny words
  - words with apostrophes

- Minimize markup. Only include markup if the student struggles. (different from TYC)

# Knowledge state helpers

- For each sound: unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent (and also notes about struggles)
- For each funny word: unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent (and also notes about struggles)
- For silent sounds: unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent
- For each capital letter: unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent (and also notes about struggles)
- For each skill (below): unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent (and also notes about struggles)
  - Reading left to right
  - Keeping track of your place in a sentence or paragraph
  - Blending sounds without stopping
  - Reading the fast way (sound out in your head)
- For each activity and item task: unstarted, introduced, struggling, slow, fluent (and also notes about struggles)
